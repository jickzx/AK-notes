# Requirements and Specifications

## Why Requirements Matter

- Bad requirements are strongly linked to over-budget projects and project failure. [L03 p10](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=10>) [L03 p13](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=13>)
- Incomplete requirements and lack of user involvement are major causes of project failure. [L03 p14](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=14>)
- Understanding stakeholder requirements is key to effective system development. [L03 p24](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=24>)
- Requirements define the project's foundation, quality target, decision basis, progress tracking basis, and testing basis. [L03 p27](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=27>)
- Finding requirements errors early is much cheaper than finding them during maintenance. [L03 p17](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=17>)

## Requirements Engineering

- Requirements engineering works out stakeholder needs and the services the product must provide to meet those needs. [L03 p27](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=27>)
- The requirements engineering process includes feasibility study, elicitation and analysis, specification, and validation. [L03 p32](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=32>)
- Outputs of requirements engineering can include feasibility reports, system models, user/system requirements, and a requirements document. [L03 p32](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=32>)
- Requirements come from briefs, clients, users, existing systems, competitor/product analysis, market segments, platform rules, and tenders. [L03 p29](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=29>) [L03 p30](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=30>)

## Elicitation and Stakeholders

- Requirements elicitation starts by identifying stakeholders from briefs, problem analysis, interviews, and discussions. [L03 p37](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=37>)
- Stakeholders can be classified as primary, secondary, and tertiary. [L03 p37](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=37>)
- Stakeholder analysis can also consider priority, importance, impact, example people, and what information is needed from them. [L03 p38](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=38>)
- Personas represent real user types and should clarify motivations, expectations, goals, knowledge, and differences between stakeholders. [L03 p40](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=40>)
- Personas help designers avoid designing only for themselves and help prioritise requirements. [L03 p43](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=43>)
- Use case diagrams represent actors and the tasks they need to perform with the system. [L03 p47](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=47>)
- Use cases can include optional extensions and mandatory related tasks. [L03 p49](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=49>)

## Requirements vs Specifications

- Requirements describe what users, clients, or stakeholders need the system to do. [L04 p3](<../Lecture Slides/04 - Requirements Gathering.pdf#page=3>)
- Specifications describe how the system will be built or structured to satisfy requirements. [L07 p4](<../Lecture Slides/07 - Specifications.pdf#page=4>)
- Functional requirements/specifications describe what the system does. [L04 p3](<../Lecture Slides/04 - Requirements Gathering.pdf#page=3>)
- Non-functional requirements/specifications describe constraints or qualities such as performance, security, dependability, or usability. [L04 p3](<../Lecture Slides/04 - Requirements Gathering.pdf#page=3>)
- Requirements elicitation data has to be converted into actual requirements before it can support later engineering work. [L04 p3](<../Lecture Slides/04 - Requirements Gathering.pdf#page=3>)

## Requirements Modelling and Validation

- Requirements models help turn observations, interviews, surveys, and user behaviour into a requirements document. [L05 p4](<../Lecture Slides/05 - Requirements Modelling.pdf#page=4>)
- Requirements models are useful because they communicate and organise stakeholder needs. [L05 p7](<../Lecture Slides/05 - Requirements Modelling.pdf#page=7>)
- UML diagrams can be used for requirements modelling and later for design/specification at a different abstraction level. [L05 p7](<../Lecture Slides/05 - Requirements Modelling.pdf#page=7>)
- Requirements documents are used by multiple audiences, including clients, developers, testers, and project managers. [L06 p4](<../Lecture Slides/06 - Requirements Validation.pdf#page=4>)
- Requirements validation checks whether requirements are correct, complete enough, consistent, feasible, and testable before later work depends on them. [L06 p4](<../Lecture Slides/06 - Requirements Validation.pdf#page=4>)

## Exam Angles

- If asked why requirements are important, say poor requirements cause failure, are expensive to fix late, and form the basis for design, testing, quality, and progress tracking. - If asked about requirements elicitation, mention stakeholder analysis, interviews/discussions, personas, and use cases. [L03 p37](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=37>) [L03 p40](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=40>) [L03 p47](<../Lecture Slides/03 - Requirements Introduction.pdf#page=47>)
- If asked about UML being used twice, explain that a diagram can represent user tasks at requirements level and software interactions/classes at specification/design level. [L05 p7](<../Lecture Slides/05 - Requirements Modelling.pdf#page=7>)
