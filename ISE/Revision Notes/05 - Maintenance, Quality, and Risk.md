# Maintenance, Quality, and Risk

## Maintenance and Evolution

- Maintenance/evolution happens after delivery and is driven by change. [L16 p6](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=6>)
- Maintenance includes correcting undiscovered errors, updating for new platforms, and enhancing services as new requirements appear. [L16 p8](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=8>)
- Change is inevitable because clients, business strategies, technologies, and user needs change over time. [L16 p9](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=9>)
- Maintenance is often the longest lifecycle phase. [L16 p8](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=8>)
- Maintenance is the most expensive stage to make changes during. [L16 p11](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=11>)
- Functionality changes are new requirements that can affect specifications, system designs, code architecture, code, and acceptance tests. [L16 p13](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=13>)
- Change should begin with requirements change management, not arbitrary code edits. [L16 p13](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=13>) [L16 p14](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=14>)
- Emergency changes should be documented and later redesigned properly if needed. [L16 p16](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=16>)
- Refactoring improves code structure to slow degradation through change and can be viewed as preventative maintenance. [L16 p18](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=18>)
- Evolution is best thought of as another software engineering loop across releases. [L16 p35](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=35>) [L16 p39](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=39>)

## Software Quality

- Software quality is not just a release or acceptance testing stage. [L17 p5](<../Lecture Slides/17 - Software Quality.pdf#page=5>)
- Quality management should be pervasive and cultural so defects are less likely to reach late testing. [L17 p5](<../Lecture Slides/17 - Software Quality.pdf#page=5>)
- QA teams plan for quality, define standards/procedures, and check projects conform to company standards. [L17 p7](<../Lecture Slides/17 - Software Quality.pdf#page=7>)
- QA teams should ideally be separate from development teams and report above the project manager. [L17 p7](<../Lecture Slides/17 - Software Quality.pdf#page=7>)
- QA includes planning for quality, defining standards, checking for quality, identifying inspection points, and identifying measures. [L17 p8](<../Lecture Slides/17 - Software Quality.pdf#page=8>)
- A quality plan defines what high-quality software means for a particular project. [L17 p12](<../Lecture Slides/17 - Software Quality.pdf#page=12>)
- Quality plans can include product introduction, product plans, process description, quality goals, and risks/risk management. [L17 p12](<../Lecture Slides/17 - Software Quality.pdf#page=12>)
- QA improves future projects by documenting what worked and standardising better processes. [L17 p14](<../Lecture Slides/17 - Software Quality.pdf#page=14>)

## Risk Management

- Software risks include security, dependability, data protection, and product-level risks. [L18 p8](<../Lecture Slides/18 - Risk Management.pdf#page=8>)
- Software risks can create new requirements, specifications, and designs. [L18 p8](<../Lecture Slides/18 - Risk Management.pdf#page=8>)
- Software risks should be analysed early, including clear "shall not" requirements where appropriate. [L18 p8](<../Lecture Slides/18 - Risk Management.pdf#page=8>)
- Project risk is about risks to delivery, planning, cost, staffing, timing, and project success. [L18 p8](<../Lecture Slides/18 - Risk Management.pdf#page=8>)
- Project risk should be analysed at the start of planning. [L18 p8](<../Lecture Slides/18 - Risk Management.pdf#page=8>)
- Risk management is not over after planning; risk monitoring continues during the project. [L18 p8](<../Lecture Slides/18 - Risk Management.pdf#page=8>)
- Risk strategies include mitigating, avoiding, and handling/contingency planning. [L18 p8](<../Lecture Slides/18 - Risk Management.pdf#page=8>)

## Exam Angles

- If asked about maintenance change types, use fault fixing/corrective, adaptation to platforms/environments, and new functionality/enhancement. [L16 p8](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=8>) [L16 p39](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=39>)
- If asked why requirements change management matters, say changes affect requirements, specs, architecture, code, and acceptance tests. [L16 p13](<../Lecture Slides/16 - Agile vs Traditional and Maintenance.pdf#page=13>)
- If asked why quality cannot just be testing, say late testing finds problems too late and quality must be built into process, standards, reviews, and planning. [L17 p5](<../Lecture Slides/17 - Software Quality.pdf#page=5>) [L17 p7](<../Lecture Slides/17 - Software Quality.pdf#page=7>)
- If asked software vs project risks, say software risks affect the product; project risks affect delivery and planning. [L18 p8](<../Lecture Slides/18 - Risk Management.pdf#page=8>)
