## 3.4 Basis and Dimension

### Basis of a Subspace

As we saw in [[3.3 Subspaces]], a [[Subspace|subspace]] is really a [[Span|span]] viewed without a particular spanning set in mind. Since a subspace usually has many different spanning sets, we want a spanning set with no redundancy.

#### Definition

Let $V$ be a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$. A [[Basis|basis]] of $V$ is a list of vectors $(\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\dots,\mathbf{v}_m)$ in $V$ such that:

1. $V = \operatorname{Span}\{\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\dots,\mathbf{v}_m\}$.
2. The set $\{\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\dots,\mathbf{v}_m\}$ is [[Linear Independence|linearly independent]].

So a basis is a spanning set with no unnecessary vectors.

Equivalently, if $(\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\dots,\mathbf{v}_m)$ is a basis of $V$, then no proper subset of these vectors can still span $V$.

#### Definition

The number of vectors in any basis of $V$ is called the [[Dimension|dimension]] of $V$, written

$$
\dim(V).
$$

Different bases of the same subspace may look different, but they always contain the same number of vectors.

### Example: A Basis of $\mathbb{R}^2$

A basis of $\mathbb{R}^2$ is

$$
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
\right\}.
$$

These vectors span $\mathbb{R}^2$ because every vector

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
a\\
b
\end{pmatrix}
$$

can be written as

$$
a
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
+
b
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

They are also linearly independent because

$$
x
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
+
y
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
$$

implies $x=y=0$.

So $\mathbb{R}^2$ has dimension $2$.

More generally, the standard coordinate vectors

$$
\mathbf{e}_1,\mathbf{e}_2,\dots,\mathbf{e}_n
$$

form the standard basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$, so

$$
\dim(\mathbb{R}^n)=n.
$$

### Basis, Spanning, and Pivots

Suppose $A$ is the matrix with columns $\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\dots,\mathbf{v}_n$.

- The set $\{\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\dots,\mathbf{v}_n\}$ spans $\mathbb{R}^n$ if and only if $A$ has a [[Pivot|pivot]] in every row.
- The set $\{\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\dots,\mathbf{v}_n\}$ is [[Linear Independence|linearly independent]] if and only if $A$ has a [[Pivot|pivot]] in every column.

When $A$ is square, these two conditions are equivalent. So for a square matrix, the columns span $\mathbb{R}^n$ if and only if they are linearly independent.

### Example: A Basis of a Plane

Let

$$
V=
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
x\\
y\\
z
\end{pmatrix}
\in \mathbb{R}^3
\mid x+3y+z=0
\right\}.
$$

Since this is the [[Solution Set|solution set]] of a homogeneous equation, it is a [[Subspace|subspace]].

Consider the vectors

$$
\mathbf{v}_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
-3\\
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf{v}_2=
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1\\
-3
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Both vectors lie in $V$ because

$$
-3+3(1)+0=0,
\qquad
0+3(1)-3=0.
$$

To show that they span $V$, let

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
x\\
y\\
z
\end{pmatrix}
\in V.
$$

Then $x+3y+z=0$, so

$$
y=-\frac{1}{3}(x+z).
$$

Therefore,

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
x\\
y\\
z
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
x\\
-\frac{1}{3}(x+z)\\
z
\end{pmatrix}
=
-\frac{x}{3}
\begin{pmatrix}
-3\\
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
-\frac{z}{3}
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1\\
-3
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

They are linearly independent because

$$
c_1
\begin{pmatrix}
-3\\
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
+
c_2
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1\\
-3
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
0\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
$$

implies $c_1=c_2=0$.

So

$$
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
-3\\
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1\\
-3
\end{pmatrix}
\right\}
$$

is a basis for $V$, and $\dim(V)=2$.

### Computing a Basis for a Column Space

To find a basis for [[Column Space|$\operatorname{Col}(A)$]], use the pivot columns of the original matrix.

#### Theorem

The pivot columns of a matrix $A$ form a basis for $\operatorname{Col}(A)$.

It is important that these are the pivot columns of the original matrix, not the pivot columns of the [[RREF]].

#### Example: A Basis of a Span

Find a basis of

$$
V=
\operatorname{Span}
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
-2\\
2
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
2\\
-3\\
4
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
4\\
0
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
-1\\
5\\
-2
\end{pmatrix}
\right\}.
$$

Let

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2 & 0 & -1\\
-2 & -3 & 4 & 5\\
2 & 4 & 0 & -2
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
\operatorname{RREF}(A)=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 & -8 & -7\\
0 & 1 & 4 & 3\\
0 & 0 & 0 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The first two columns are pivot columns, so a basis for $V$ is

$$
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
-2\\
2
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
2\\
-3\\
4
\end{pmatrix}
\right\}.
$$

So $\dim(V)=2$.

### Computing a Basis for a Null Space

To find a basis for [[Null Space|$\operatorname{Nul}(A)$]]:

1. Solve the homogeneous equation $A\mathbf{x}=0$.
2. Write the solution in [[2.3 Parametric Form|parametric vector form]].
3. The vectors attached to the [[Free Variable|free variables]] form a basis for $\operatorname{Nul}(A)$.

#### Example

Let

$$
V=
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
x\\
y\\
z
\end{pmatrix}
\in\mathbb{R}^3
\mid x+2y=z
\right\}.
$$

Then

$$
x+2y-z=0,
$$

so

$$
V=\operatorname{Nul}
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2 & -1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The equation becomes

$$
x=-2y+z.
$$

So the parametric vector form is

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
x\\
y\\
z
\end{pmatrix}
=
y
\begin{pmatrix}
-2\\
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
+
z
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Therefore a basis for $V$ is

$$
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
-2\\
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
\right\},
$$

and $\dim(V)=2$.

### The Basis Theorem

Let $V$ be a subspace with $\dim(V)=m$.

#### Basis Theorem

- Any $m$ linearly independent vectors in $V$ form a basis for $V$.
- Any $m$ vectors that span $V$ form a basis for $V$.

So if you already know the dimension of a subspace, then to prove that a list of $m$ vectors is a basis, it is enough to check only one of:

1. the vectors are linearly independent
2. the vectors span $V$

because the other property will follow automatically.

Equivalently, if $B=(\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\dots,\mathbf{v}_m)$ is a list of vectors in $V$, then any two of the following imply the third:

1. $B$ is linearly independent
2. $B$ spans $V$
3. $\dim(V)=m$

### Example: Two Noncollinear Vectors in a Plane

Suppose $V$ is a plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$, so $\dim(V)=2$.

Then any two noncollinear vectors in $V$ form a basis for $V$.

For instance, if

$$
\mathbf{w}_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
3\\
-5\\
6
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\mathbf{w}_2=
\begin{pmatrix}
-2\\
5\\
-4
\end{pmatrix},
$$

and both lie in $V$, then they form a basis as long as they are not scalar multiples of each other.

This is an efficient way to build a basis once the dimension is already known.
