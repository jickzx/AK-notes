## 3.6 The Rank Theorem

### Rank and Nullity

Let $A$ be an $m\times n$ matrix.

#### Definition

The [[Rank|rank]] of $A$ is

$$
\operatorname{rank}(A)=\dim(\operatorname{Col}(A)).
$$

So the rank is the number of [[Pivot|pivot]] columns of $A$.

#### Definition

The [[Nullity|nullity]] of $A$ is

$$
\operatorname{nullity}(A)=\dim(\operatorname{Nul}(A)).
$$

So the nullity is the number of [[Free Variable|free variables]] in the homogeneous system $A\mathbf{x}=0$.

### The Rank Theorem

If $A$ has $n$ columns, then

$$
\operatorname{rank}(A)+\operatorname{nullity}(A)=n.
$$

This is the [[Rank|rank theorem]].

The reason is simple:

- every column is either a pivot column or a non-pivot column
- pivot columns contribute to the rank
- non-pivot columns correspond to free variables, which contribute to the nullity

So the total number of columns splits into

$$
\text{number of pivots}+\text{number of free variables}.
$$

### Example

Let

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & -1 & 2\\
-2 & 2 & -4
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Its [[RREF]] is

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & -1 & 2\\
0 & 0 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

There is one pivot column, so

$$
\operatorname{rank}(A)=1.
$$

There are two free variables, so

$$
\operatorname{nullity}(A)=2.
$$

Since $A$ has $3$ columns,

$$
1+2=3,
$$

which matches the theorem.

### Geometric Meaning

The [[Column Space|column space]] measures how many independent output directions the matrix has, while the [[Null Space|null space]] measures how many input directions collapse to zero.

The rank theorem says these two dimensions always balance to the total number of columns.

### Consequences

- If $A$ has no free variables, then $\operatorname{nullity}(A)=0$ and the columns are [[Linear Independence|linearly independent]].
- If $A$ has a pivot in every column, then $\operatorname{rank}(A)=n$.
- If $A$ has fewer pivots, then the missing pivots appear as free variables in $A\mathbf{x}=0$.
