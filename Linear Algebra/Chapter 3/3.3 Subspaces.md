## 3.3 Subspaces

### Definition and Examples

In this section we discuss [[Subspace|subspaces]] of $\mathbb{R}^n$. A subspace turns out to be exactly the same thing as a [[Span|span]], except we do not begin with a particular spanning set in mind.

For example, the solution set of the equation

$$
x + 3y + z = 0
$$

is a span because the equation is homogeneous, but we would have to compute the parametric vector form in order to write it explicitly as a span.

#### Definition

See [[Subspace]] for the core definition and the three defining properties.

A subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is any collection of points of $\mathbb{R}^n$.

For instance, the unit circle

$$
C = \{(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \mid x^2 + y^2 = 1\}
$$

is a subset of $\mathbb{R}^2$.

#### Examples

- $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a subspace of itself.
- $\{0\}$ is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$.
- A line through the origin is a subspace.
- A plane through the origin is a subspace.

#### Non-examples

- A line not containing the origin is not a subspace.
- The unit circle is not a subspace.
- The first quadrant in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is not a subspace because it is not closed under scalar multiplication by negative numbers.
- The union of a line and a plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is not a subspace because it is not closed under addition.

### Verifying That a Subset Is a Subspace

To show that a subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a subspace, you have to check the three defining properties.

#### Example

Let

$$
V =
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
a\\
b
\end{pmatrix}
\in \mathbb{R}^2 \mid 2a = 3b
\right\}.
$$

We verify that $V$ is a subspace.

- The zero vector is in $V$ because $2\cdot 0 = 3\cdot 0$.
- If
  $$
  \mathbf{u}=
  \begin{pmatrix}
  a\\
  b
  \end{pmatrix},
  \quad
  \mathbf{v}=
  \begin{pmatrix}
  c\\
  d
  \end{pmatrix}
  \in V,
  $$
  then $2a=3b$ and $2c=3d$. So
  $$
  \mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}=
  \begin{pmatrix}
  a+c\\
  b+d
  \end{pmatrix}
  $$
  also satisfies
  $$
  2(a+c)=2a+2c=3b+3d=3(b+d),
  $$
  so $\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}\in V$.
- If
  $$
  \mathbf{v}=
  \begin{pmatrix}
  a\\
  b
  \end{pmatrix}\in V
  $$
  and $c\in\mathbb{R}$, then
  $$
  c\mathbf{v}=
  \begin{pmatrix}
  ca\\
  cb
  \end{pmatrix}
  $$
  satisfies
  $$
  2(ca)=c(2a)=c(3b)=3(cb),
  $$
  so $c\mathbf{v}\in V$.

Therefore $V$ is a subspace. In fact, it is the line through the origin with slope $\frac{2}{3}$.

#### Example: Showing a Subset Is Not a Subspace

Let

$$
V =
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
a\\
b
\end{pmatrix}
\in \mathbb{R}^2 \mid ab = 0
\right\}.
$$

This set contains the zero vector and is closed under scalar multiplication, but it is not closed under addition. For example,

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
\in V,
\qquad
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
\in V,
$$

but

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
+
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
\notin V.
$$

So $V$ is not a subspace.

### Spans, Column Spaces, and Null Spaces

#### Theorem

If $\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\dots,\mathbf{v}_p$ are any vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$, then $\operatorname{Span}\{\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\dots,\mathbf{v}_p\}$ is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$.

Moreover, any subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$ can be written as the span of a linearly independent set of vectors.

So:

- [[Span|spans]] are [[Subspace|subspaces]]
- and subspaces are really spans viewed from a different perspective

#### Definition

Let $A$ be an $m \times n$ matrix.

- The [[Column Space|column space]] of $A$ is the subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$ spanned by the columns of $A$. It is written $\operatorname{Col}(A)$.
- The [[Null Space|null space]] of $A$ is the subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$ consisting of all solutions of the homogeneous equation $A\mathbf{x}=0$:
  $$
  \operatorname{Nul}(A)=\{\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^n \mid A\mathbf{x}=0\}.
  $$

The column space is defined as a span, so it is automatically a subspace. The null space is also a subspace because it is the [[Solution Set|solution set]] of a homogeneous system.

### Example: Column Space and Null Space

Describe the [[Column Space|column space]] and the [[Null Space|null space]] of

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1\\
1 & 1\\
1 & 1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

#### Solution

The column space is the span of the columns:

$$
\operatorname{Col}(A)=
\operatorname{Span}
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
1\\
1
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
1\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
\right\}
=
\operatorname{Span}
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
1\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
\right\}.
$$

This is a line in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

To compute the null space, row reduce:

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1\\
1 & 1\\
1 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\xrightarrow{\text{RREF}}
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1\\
0 & 0\\
0 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

This gives the equation

$$
x+y=0.
$$

So

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
x &= -y\\
y &= y
\end{aligned}
\right.
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\begin{pmatrix}
x\\
y
\end{pmatrix}
=
y
\begin{pmatrix}
-1\\
1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Hence

$$
\operatorname{Nul}(A)=
\operatorname{Span}
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
-1\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
\right\},
$$

which is a line in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

Notice that the column space is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$, while the null space is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^2$, because $A$ has three rows and two columns.

### Null Spaces Are Solution Sets

The [[Null Space|null space]] of a matrix is the [[Solution Set|solution set]] of a homogeneous system of equations.

For example, the null space of

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 7 & 2\\
-2 & 1 & 3\\
4 & -2 & -3
\end{pmatrix}
$$

is the solution set of

$$
A\mathbf{x}=0,
$$

that is, the system

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
x + 7y + 2z &= 0\\
-2x + y + 3z &= 0\\
4x - 2y - 3z &= 0.
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$

Conversely, the solution set of any homogeneous system is precisely the null space of the corresponding coefficient matrix.

### Recipe: Compute a Spanning Set for a Null Space

To find a spanning set for $\operatorname{Nul}(A)$:

1. Solve the homogeneous equation $A\mathbf{x}=0$.
2. Compute the parametric vector form.
3. The vectors attached to the [[Free Variable|free variables]] form a spanning set for $\operatorname{Nul}(A)$.

#### Example: Two Free Variables

Find a spanning set for the null space of

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
2 & 3 & -8 & -5\\
-1 & 2 & -3 & -8
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The [[RREF]] is

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 & -1 & 2\\
0 & 1 & -2 & -3
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The free variables are $x_3$ and $x_4$, so

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
x_1 &= x_3 - 2x_4\\
x_2 &= 2x_3 + 3x_4\\
x_3 &= x_3\\
x_4 &= x_4
\end{aligned}
\right.
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\begin{pmatrix}
x_1\\
x_2\\
x_3\\
x_4
\end{pmatrix}
=
x_3
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
2\\
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
+
x_4
\begin{pmatrix}
-2\\
3\\
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\operatorname{Nul}(A)=
\operatorname{Span}
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
2\\
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
-2\\
3\\
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
\right\}.
$$

#### Example: No Free Variables

Find a spanning set for the null space of

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 3\\
2 & 4
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The [[RREF]] is

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0\\
0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

There are no [[Free Variable|free variables]], so the only solution of $A\mathbf{x}=0$ is the trivial solution. Hence

$$
\operatorname{Nul}(A)=\{0\}.
$$

It is natural to write this as

$$
\operatorname{Span}\{\emptyset\} = \{0\}.
$$

### Writing a Subspace as a Column Space or Null Space

When asking questions about a subspace, it is usually best to rewrite the subspace as a [[Column Space|column space]] or a [[Null Space|null space]].

If your subset is already a column space or a null space of a matrix, then you automatically know it is a subspace.

#### Example

Let

$$
V=
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
a\\
b
\end{pmatrix}
\in\mathbb{R}^2 \mid 2a = 3b
\right\}.
$$

This is exactly the solution set of the homogeneous equation

$$
2x - 3y = 0.
$$

Therefore,

$$
V=\operatorname{Nul}\begin{pmatrix}2 & -3\end{pmatrix}.
$$

In particular, it is a subspace.

#### Example

Let

$$
V=
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
2x+y\\
x-y\\
3x-2y
\end{pmatrix}
\mid x,y\in\mathbb{R}
\right\}.
$$

Then

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
2x+y\\
x-y\\
3x-2y
\end{pmatrix}
=
x
\begin{pmatrix}
2\\
1\\
3
\end{pmatrix}
+
y
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
-1\\
-2
\end{pmatrix},
$$

so

$$
V=
\operatorname{Col}
\begin{pmatrix}
2 & 1\\
1 & -1\\
3 & -2
\end{pmatrix}.
$$
