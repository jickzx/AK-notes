## 3.5 Bases as Coordinate Systems

### Coordinates Relative to a Basis

If $B=(\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\dots,\mathbf{v}_m)$ is a [[Basis|basis]] for a subspace $V$, then every vector $\mathbf{x}\in V$ can be written in exactly one way as

$$
\mathbf{x}=c_1\mathbf{v}_1+c_2\mathbf{v}_2+\cdots+c_m\mathbf{v}_m.
$$

The coefficients are unique because a basis both spans and is [[Linear Independence|linearly independent]].

#### Definition

The coefficients $c_1,c_2,\dots,c_m$ are the [[B-coordinates|$B$-coordinates]] of $\mathbf{x}$.

The coordinate vector of $\mathbf{x}$ relative to $B$ is

$$
[\mathbf{x}]_B=
\begin{pmatrix}
c_1\\
c_2\\
\vdots\\
c_m
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

### Example

Let

$$
B=
\left(
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
1
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
\right).
$$

To find the $B$-coordinates of

$$
\mathbf{x}=
\begin{pmatrix}
3\\
5
\end{pmatrix},
$$

write

$$
\mathbf{x}
=
c_1
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
+
c_2
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Comparing coordinates gives

$$
c_1=3,
\qquad
c_1+c_2=5,
$$

so $c_2=2$. Therefore

$$
[\mathbf{x}]_B=
\begin{pmatrix}
3\\
2
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

### Changing Between Coordinate Systems

If $B$ is the matrix whose columns are the basis vectors $\mathbf{v}_1,\dots,\mathbf{v}_m$, then

$$
\mathbf{x}=B[\mathbf{x}]_B.
$$

So:

- multiplying by $B$ converts from $B$-coordinates to standard coordinates
- multiplying by $B^{-1}$ converts from standard coordinates to $B$-coordinates, when $B$ is square and invertible

### Recipe: Find $B$-Coordinates

To compute $[\mathbf{x}]_B$:

1. Write $\mathbf{x}$ as a linear combination of the basis vectors.
2. Read off the coefficients.
3. Place those coefficients in a column vector.

### Recipe: Recover the Usual Coordinates

If you are given $[\mathbf{x}]_B$, then recover $\mathbf{x}$ by forming the same linear combination of the basis vectors.

Equivalently, if $B$ is the matrix with basis vectors as columns, then

$$
\mathbf{x}=B[\mathbf{x}]_B.
$$

### Why This Matters

A [[Basis|basis]] gives a coordinate system on a subspace. Different bases give different coordinate descriptions of the same vector.

This becomes especially important in [[4.7 Invertible Matrices and Coordinate Systems]], where linear transformations are represented with respect to chosen bases.
