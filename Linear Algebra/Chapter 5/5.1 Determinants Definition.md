## 5.1 Determinants: Definition

### Definition

The [[Determinant|determinant]] of a square matrix $A$ is a scalar $\det(A)$ that records how the matrix changes area or volume and how it behaves under row operations.

For elementary row operations:

1. Replacing a row by itself plus a multiple of another row does not change the determinant.
2. Multiplying a row by $c$ multiplies the determinant by $c$.
3. Swapping two rows multiplies the determinant by $-1$.
4. $\det(I_n)=1$.

### Quick Consequences

- If a row is all zeros, then $\det(A)=0$.
- If two rows are equal, then $\det(A)=0$.
- If one row is a multiple of another, then $\det(A)=0$.
- If $A$ is triangular, then $\det(A)$ is the product of the diagonal entries.
- A matrix is invertible exactly when $\det(A)\neq 0$.

### Worked Example

Let

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
2 & 1\\
0 & 3
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

This matrix is upper triangular, so

$$
\det(A)=2\cdot 3=6.
$$

For a less immediate example, consider

$$
B=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2\\
3 & 4
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Using the $2\times 2$ formula,

$$
\det(B)=1\cdot 4-2\cdot 3=-2.
$$

Since the determinant is nonzero, $B$ is invertible.

### Why This Matters

The determinant packages several important facts into one number:

- invertibility;
- whether rows or columns are linearly dependent;
- how much a matrix transformation scales area or volume.

### Revision Cues

- Determinant zero means the transformation squashes space into a lower dimension.
- Determinant nonzero means the columns are independent and the matrix is invertible.
- Triangular matrices are the fastest determinant computations.
