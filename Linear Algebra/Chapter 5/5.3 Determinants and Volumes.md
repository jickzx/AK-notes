## 5.3 Determinants and Volumes

### Geometric Meaning

For a square matrix $A$, the absolute value

$$
|\det(A)|
$$

is the factor by which the matrix transformation $T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}$ scales area or volume.

So:

- in $\mathbb{R}^2$, $|\det(A)|$ is the area scale factor;
- in $\mathbb{R}^3$, $|\det(A)|$ is the volume scale factor.

### Sign of the Determinant

- If $\det(A)>0$, orientation is preserved.
- If $\det(A)<0$, orientation is reversed.
- If $\det(A)=0$, the image collapses into a lower-dimensional set.

### Worked Example

Let

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2\\
2 & 1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
\det(A)=1\cdot 1-2\cdot 2=-3.
$$

So the transformation scales area by a factor of

$$
|\det(A)|=3
$$

and reverses orientation because the determinant is negative.

### Why This Explains Invertibility

If $\det(A)=0$, then the transformation crushes area or volume down to zero. That means different inputs can land on the same output, so the transformation cannot be inverted.

If $\det(A)\neq 0$, then the transformation does not collapse space, so inversion is possible.

### Revision Cues

- Determinant tells you more than just invertibility; it gives a geometric scaling factor.
- Absolute value gives the size of scaling.
- Sign tells you whether orientation flips.
