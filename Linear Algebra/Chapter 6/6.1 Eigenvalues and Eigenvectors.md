## 6.1 Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

### Definition

Let $A$ be a square matrix. A nonzero vector $\mathbf{x}$ is an [[Eigenvector|eigenvector]] of $A$ if

$$
A\mathbf{x}=\lambda \mathbf{x}
$$

for some scalar $\lambda$.

That scalar $\lambda$ is the corresponding [[Eigenvalue|eigenvalue]].

### Intuition

Most vectors change direction when multiplied by a matrix. Eigenvectors are the special directions that do not change direction; they are only stretched, shrunk, or reversed.

### Worked Example

Let

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
3 & 0\\
0 & 2
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
A
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
3\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
=
3
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix},
$$

so $\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $3$.

Also,

$$
A
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
2
\end{pmatrix}
=
2
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
1
\end{pmatrix},
$$

so $\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $2$.

### How To Find Them

To find eigenvalues and eigenvectors:

1. Solve $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$ for $\lambda$.
2. For each eigenvalue, solve
   $$
   (A-\lambda I)\mathbf{x}=0
   $$
   to get the corresponding eigenvectors.

The set of all eigenvectors for a fixed eigenvalue $\lambda$, together with the zero vector, forms its eigenspace.
The dimension of that eigenspace is the [[Geometric Multiplicity|geometric multiplicity]] of $\lambda$.

### Why This Matters

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors control:

- [[Diagonalisation|diagonalisation]];
- powers of matrices;
- long-term behaviour in [[7.3 Discrete Dynamical Systems|discrete dynamical systems]];
- [[7.4 Stochastic Matrices and the Steady State|steady states]].
