## 6.4 Diagonalisation

### Definition

A matrix $A$ is [[Diagonalisation|diagonalisable]] if it is similar to a diagonal matrix:

$$
A=PDP^{-1}.
$$

where

$$
P \text{ is invertible}, \qquad
D=
\begin{pmatrix}
{\color{orange} *} & 0 & \cdots & 0\\
0 & {\color{orange} *} & \cdots & 0\\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\
0 & 0 & \cdots & {\color{orange} *}
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Here the columns of $P$ are eigenvectors of $A$, and the diagonal entries of $D$ are the corresponding eigenvalues.

So if

$$
P=
\begin{pmatrix}
\vert & \vert &  & \vert\\
\mathbf{v}_1 & \mathbf{v}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{v}_n\\
\vert & \vert &  & \vert
\end{pmatrix},
$$

then each column $\mathbf{v}_i$ is an eigenvector of $A$, and the matching diagonal entry of $D$ is its eigenvalue $\lambda_i$.

That means $P$ is the change-of-basis matrix into the eigenvector basis.

### Why Diagonal Form Helps

Powers of diagonal matrices are easy:

$$
D^k=
\begin{pmatrix}
\lambda_1^k & 0 & \cdots & 0\\
0 & \lambda_2^k & \cdots & 0\\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\
0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda_n^k
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

So if $A=PDP^{-1}$, then

$$
A^k=PD^kP^{-1}.
$$

### Worked Example

Let

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 4\\
2 & 3
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

First find the eigenvalues from

$$
\det(A-\lambda I)=0.
$$

Here

$$
A-\lambda I=
\begin{pmatrix}
1-\lambda & 4\\
2 & 3-\lambda
\end{pmatrix},
$$

so

$$
\det(A-\lambda I)
=(1-\lambda)(3-\lambda)-8
=\lambda^2-4\lambda-5.
$$

Solving

$$
\lambda^2-4\lambda-5=0
$$

gives the eigenvalues

$$
\lambda_1=5,
\qquad
\lambda_2=-1.
$$

Now find an eigenvector for each eigenvalue.

For $\lambda_1=5$:

$$
A-5I=
\begin{pmatrix}
-4 & 4\\
2 & -2
\end{pmatrix}
\sim
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & -1\\
0 & 0
\end{pmatrix},
$$

so $x_1=x_2$, and an eigenvector is

$$
\mathbf{v}_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

For $\lambda_2=-1$:

$$
A+I=
\begin{pmatrix}
2 & 4\\
2 & 4
\end{pmatrix}
\sim
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2\\
0 & 0
\end{pmatrix},
$$

so $x_1=-2x_2$, and an eigenvector is

$$
\mathbf{v}_2=
\begin{pmatrix}
-2\\
1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Now build $P$ from the eigenvectors and $D$ from the matching eigenvalues:

$$
P=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & -2\\
1 & 1
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
D=
\begin{pmatrix}
5 & 0\\
0 & -1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Since $\mathbf{v}_1$ and $\mathbf{v}_2$ are linearly independent, $P$ is invertible, so $A$ is diagonalisable.

So the diagonalisation process is:

1. Start with the matrix $A$.
2. Find its eigenvalues.
3. Find an eigenvector for each eigenvalue.
4. Build $P$ by putting those eigenvectors as the columns.
5. Build $D$ by putting the matching eigenvalues on the diagonal in the same order.

So $P$ comes from the eigenvectors, and $D$ comes from the eigenvalues.

### Key Test

A matrix is diagonalisable if it has enough linearly independent eigenvectors to form a basis of the space.

For an $n\times n$ matrix, that means $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors.

### Why This Matters

Diagonalisation is the main computational payoff of the eigenvalue topic. It simplifies:

- matrix powers;
- dynamical systems;
- steady-state calculations.
