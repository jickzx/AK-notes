## 6.3 Similarity

### Definition

Two square matrices $A$ and $B$ are [[Similarity|similar]] if there exists an invertible matrix $P$ such that

$$
A=PBP^{-1}.
$$

### Intuition

Similar matrices represent the same linear transformation, but in different coordinate systems.

The matrix changes because the basis changes, not because the transformation itself changes.

### Worked Example

Let

$$
B=
\begin{pmatrix}
2 & 0\\
0 & 3
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
P=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1\\
0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Since

$$
P^{-1}=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & -1\\
0 & 1
\end{pmatrix},
$$

we get

$$
A=PBP^{-1}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
2 & 1\\
0 & 3
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

So $A$ and $B$ are similar even though they do not look the same.

### What Similar Matrices Share

Similar matrices always have the same:

- eigenvalues;
- characteristic polynomial;
- determinant;
- [[Trace|trace]].

They do not necessarily have the same entries or the same eigenvectors in standard coordinates.

### Why This Matters

Similarity is the bridge to [[Diagonalisation|diagonalisation]], where we try to replace a complicated matrix by a diagonal one in a better basis.
