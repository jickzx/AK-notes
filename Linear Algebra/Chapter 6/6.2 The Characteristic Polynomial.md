## 6.2 The Characteristic Polynomial

### Definition

The [[Characteristic Polynomial|characteristic polynomial]] of a square matrix $A$ is

$$
p_A(\lambda)=\det(A-\lambda I).
$$

Its roots are exactly the [[Eigenvalue|eigenvalues]] of $A$.

For a $2\times 2$ matrix, a useful shortcut is

$$
p_A(\lambda)=\lambda^2-\operatorname{tr}(A)\lambda+\det(A).
$$

Here $\operatorname{tr}(A)$ is the [[Trace|trace]] of $A$.

### Worked Example

Let

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
4 & 1\\
0 & 2
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
A-\lambda I=
\begin{pmatrix}
4-\lambda & 1\\
0 & 2-\lambda
\end{pmatrix},
$$

so

$$
p_A(\lambda)=\det(A-\lambda I)=(4-\lambda)(2-\lambda).
$$

Therefore the eigenvalues are

$$
\lambda=4 \quad \text{and} \quad \lambda=2.
$$

### Key Facts

- An $n\times n$ matrix has a characteristic polynomial of degree $n$.
- Triangular matrices have eigenvalues equal to their diagonal entries.
- Repeated roots are possible, so an eigenvalue may have multiplicity greater than $1$.
- The [[Algebraic Multiplicity|algebraic multiplicity]] of an eigenvalue is how many times it appears as a root of the characteristic polynomial.
- The [[Geometric Multiplicity|geometric multiplicity]] of an eigenvalue is the dimension of its eigenspace.
- For every eigenvalue, geometric multiplicity is less than or equal to algebraic multiplicity.

### Why This Matters

The characteristic polynomial turns the eigenvalue problem into a determinant problem, which is often much easier to solve.
