## 6.5 Complex Eigenvalues

### Main Idea

A real matrix can have [[Eigenvalue|complex eigenvalues]] even when all of its entries are real.

This happens when the [[Characteristic Polynomial|characteristic polynomial]] has no real roots.

### Worked Example

Let

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & -1\\
1 & 1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
A-\lambda I=
\begin{pmatrix}
1-\lambda & -1\\
1 & 1-\lambda
\end{pmatrix},
$$

so

$$
\det(A-\lambda I)=(1-\lambda)^2+1
=\lambda^2-2\lambda+2.
$$

Solving

$$
\lambda^2-2\lambda+2=0
$$

gives

$$
\lambda=1\pm i.
$$

### Finding a Complex Eigenvector

Take the eigenvalue

$$
\lambda=1+i.
$$

Then

$$
A-(1+i)I=
\begin{pmatrix}
-i & -1\\
1 & -i
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

To find an eigenvector, solve

$$
(A-(1+i)I)\mathbf{x}=0.
$$

This gives

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
-i & -1\\
1 & -i
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
x_1\\
x_2
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0\\
0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

From the first row,

$$
-ix_1-x_2=0
\quad \Rightarrow \quad
x_2=-ix_1.
$$

Let $x_1=1$. Then

$$
x_2=-i,
$$

so one eigenvector is

$$
\mathbf{v}=
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
-i
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

For the conjugate eigenvalue $\lambda=1-i$, the corresponding eigenvector is

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
i
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

### Geometric Meaning

Complex eigenvalues usually indicate rotation together with scaling.

For eigenvalues

$$
a\pm bi,
$$

the scaling factor is

$$
\sqrt{a^2+b^2}.
$$

If $b\neq 0$, then there is a genuine rotational component.

### Why This Matters

Complex eigenvalues explain why some real matrices have no real eigenvectors: a pure rotation in the plane does not leave any real nonzero direction fixed.
