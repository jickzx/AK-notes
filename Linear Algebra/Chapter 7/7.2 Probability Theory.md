## 7.2 Probability Theory

### Basic Definitions

- A sample space is the set of all possible outcomes.
- An event is a subset of the sample space.
- A probability assigns a number between $0$ and $1$ to each event.

### Conditional Probability

If $P(B)\neq 0$, then

$$
P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}.
$$

This means "the probability of $A$ given that $B$ has happened."

### Bayes' Rule

$$
P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(B\mid A)P(A)}{P(B)}.
$$

Bayes' rule reverses conditioning and is useful when one direction is easier to compute than the other.

### Independence

Events $A$ and $B$ are independent if

$$
P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B).
$$

### Worked Example

Suppose a fair die is rolled.

Let

- $A$ = "the roll is even";
- $B$ = "the roll is greater than $3$".

Then

$$
P(A)=\frac{3}{6}=\frac{1}{2},
\qquad
P(B)=\frac{3}{6}=\frac{1}{2},
$$

and

$$
P(A\cap B)=P(\{4,6\})=\frac{2}{6}=\frac{1}{3}.
$$

So

$$
P(A\mid B)=\frac{1/3}{1/2}=\frac{2}{3}.
$$

### Random Variables

A random variable assigns a real number to each outcome.

Its expectation is the weighted average of its possible values.

### Why This Matters Here

This chapter supplies the language for stochastic systems, where probabilities are updated by multiplying by a [[Stochastic Matrix|stochastic matrix]].
