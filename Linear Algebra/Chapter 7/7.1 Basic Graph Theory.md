## 7.1 Basic Graph Theory

### Basic Objects

A graph consists of vertices connected by edges.

If a graph has vertices $v_1,\dots,v_n$, its [[Adjacency Matrix|adjacency matrix]] $A$ records which vertices are connected:

$$
a_{ij}=
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if there is an edge from } v_j \text{ to } v_i,\\
0 & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
$$

### Worked Example

Suppose $v_1$ is connected to $v_2$, $v_2$ is connected to $v_1$ and $v_3$, and $v_3$ is connected to $v_2$.

Then

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & 1 & 0\\
1 & 0 & 1\\
0 & 1 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

### Why Linear Algebra Appears

[[Adjacency Matrix|Adjacency matrices]] let us use matrix multiplication to count paths and study long-term behaviour on graphs.

For example, entries of $A^2$ count the number of length-$2$ paths between vertices.

### Why This Matters

Graph ideas connect naturally to [[7.3 Discrete Dynamical Systems|discrete dynamical systems]] and [[7.4 Stochastic Matrices and the Steady State|stochastic matrices]], where movement between states is described by matrices.
