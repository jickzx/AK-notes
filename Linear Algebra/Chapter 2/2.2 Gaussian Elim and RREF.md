## REF
[[REF|Row Echelon Form]] makes systems of equations easier to deal with and solve by applying three operations in some sequence.

### Augmented Matrices and Row Operations

Solving equations by elimination requires writing the variables $x$, $y$, $z$ and the equals sign over and over again, even though only the coefficients change. We can collect just the coefficients into an augmented matrix:

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
x + 2y + 3z &= 6 \\
2x - 3y + 2z &= 14 \\
3x + y - z &= -2
\end{aligned}
\right.
\quad \longrightarrow \quad
\left(
\begin{array}{ccc|c}
1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\
2 & -3 & 2 & 14 \\
3 & 1 & -1 & -2
\end{array}
\right)
$$

An [[Augmented Matrix|augmented matrix]] keeps the coefficient matrix together with the constants column. The three valid row operations are:

- `Scaling`: multiply all entries in a row by a nonzero number.
- `Replacement`: add a multiple of one row to another row.
- `Swap`: interchange two rows.

#### Scaling

$$
\left(
\begin{array}{ccc|c}
1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\
2 & -3 & 2 & 14 \\
3 & 1 & -1 & -2
\end{array}
\right)
\xrightarrow{R_1 = R_1 \times (-3)}
\left(
\begin{array}{ccc|c}
-3 & -6 & -9 & -18 \\
2 & -3 & 2 & 14 \\
3 & 1 & -1 & -2
\end{array}
\right)
$$

Here $R_1$ means "the first row", and similarly for $R_2$, $R_3$, and so on.

#### Replacement

$$
\left(
\begin{array}{ccc|c}
1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\
2 & -3 & 2 & 14 \\
3 & 1 & -1 & -2
\end{array}
\right)
\xrightarrow{R_2 = R_2 - 2R_1}
\left(
\begin{array}{ccc|c}
1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\
0 & -7 & -4 & 2 \\
3 & 1 & -1 & -2
\end{array}
\right)
$$

#### Swap

$$
\left(
\begin{array}{ccc|c}
1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\
2 & -3 & 2 & 14 \\
3 & 1 & -1 & -2
\end{array}
\right)
\xrightarrow{R_1 \leftrightarrow R_3}
\left(
\begin{array}{ccc|c}
3 & 1 & -1 & -2 \\
2 & -3 & 2 & 14 \\
1 & 2 & 3 & 6
\end{array}
\right)
$$

### What REF Looks Like for a Consistent System

In row echelon form:

$$
\left(
\begin{array}{ccccc}
{\color{orange}\star} & \star & \star & \star & \star \\
0 & {\color{orange}\star} & \star & \star & \star \\
0 & 0 & 0 & {\color{orange}\star} & \star \\
0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0
\end{array}
\right)
$$

where:

- $\star$ means any number.
- A leading nonzero entry marks a [[Pivot|pivot]] position.
- Each [[Pivot|pivot]] is to the right of the [[Pivot|pivot]] in the row above it.
- All zero rows are at the bottom.

### What RREF Looks Like for a Consistent System

In reduced row echelon form:

$$
\left(
\begin{array}{ccccc}
{\color{orange}1} & 0 & \star & 0 & \star \\
0 & {\color{orange}1} & \star & 0 & \star \\
0 & 0 & 0 & {\color{orange}1} & \star \\
0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0
\end{array}
\right)
$$

where:

- $\star$ means any number.
- Each [[Pivot|pivot]] is equal to $1$.
- Every [[Pivot|pivot]] is the only nonzero entry in its column.

### Inconsistent System in RREF

An [[Inconsistent System|inconsistent system]] shows up as a row like:

$$
\left(
\begin{array}{cccc|c}
1 & 0 & \star & \star & 0 \\
0 & 1 & \star & \star & 0 \\
{\color{red}0} & {\color{red}0} & {\color{red}0} & {\color{red}0} & {\color{red}1}
\end{array}
\right)
$$

The last row corresponds to

$$
{\color{red}0 = 1}
$$

which is impossible, so the system has no solution.

### Number of Solutions from RREF

For a linear system, the [[RREF]] tells you how many solutions there are:

1. If the last column is a [[Pivot|pivot]] column, the system is [[Inconsistent System|inconsistent]], so there are `no solutions`.
2. If every variable column is a [[Pivot|pivot]] column and the last column is not, the system has a `unique solution`.
3. If the last column is not a [[Pivot|pivot]] column and at least one variable column is not a [[Pivot|pivot]] column either, then there is at least one [[Free Variable|free variable]], so the system has `infinitely many solutions`.

So the quick test is:

- [[Pivot|pivot]] in the last column $\to$ no solution
- [[Pivot|pivots]] in every variable column $\to$ unique solution
- missing [[Pivot|pivot]] in some variable column $\to$ [[Free Variable|free variable]] $\to$ infinitely many solutions

## Gaussian Elimination

Use any combination of the three allowed row operations until the matrix is in echelon form, and then continue to reduced echelon form if needed. When a system has [[Free Variable|free variables]], the next step is to write the answer in [[Linear Algebra/Chapter 2/2.3 Parametric Form]].

### Remark: Why the Word "Pivot"?

Consider the system

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
x - y &= 0 \\
x + y &= 2
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$

Subtract the first equation from the second:

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
x - y &= 0 \\
2y &= 2
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$

Then scale the second equation:

$$
\left\{
\begin{aligned}
x - y &= 0 \\
y &= 1
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$

In matrix form:

$$
\left(
\begin{array}{cc|c}
1 & -1 & 0 \\
1 & 1 & 2
\end{array}
\right)
\xrightarrow{R_2 = R_2 - R_1}
\left(
\begin{array}{cc|c}
1 & -1 & 0 \\
0 & 2 & 2
\end{array}
\right)
\xrightarrow{R_2 = \frac{1}{2}R_2}
\left(
\begin{array}{cc|c}
1 & -1 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & 1
\end{array}
\right)
$$

Geometrically, the original line $x + y = 2$ is replaced by the line $y = 1$, while the solution point stays the same. That is the intuition behind the term [[Pivot|`pivot`]]: one equation effectively rotates into a simpler position around the shared solution.
