## 4.6 The Invertible Matrix Theorem

### The Theorem

Let $A$ be an $n\times n$ matrix, and let

$$
T:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^n,
\qquad
T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}.
$$

Then the following statements are equivalent:

1. $A$ is invertible.
2. $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ has a unique solution for every $\mathbf{b}\in\mathbb{R}^n$.
3. $A\mathbf{x}=0$ has only the trivial solution.
4. The columns of $A$ are [[Linear Independence|linearly independent]].
5. The columns of $A$ span $\mathbb{R}^n$.
6. $A$ has a [[Pivot|pivot]] in every row.
7. $A$ has a pivot in every column.
8. $\operatorname{Col}(A)=\mathbb{R}^n$.
9. $\operatorname{Nul}(A)=\{\mathbf{0}\}$.
10. The matrix transformation $T$ is [[One-to-one Transformation|one-to-one]].
11. The matrix transformation $T$ is [[Onto Transformation|onto]].

### Why It Matters

This theorem connects nearly everything we have studied so far:

- pivots
- [[Linear Independence|linear independence]]
- [[Span|spanning]]
- [[Column Space|column space]]
- [[Null Space|null space]]
- one-to-one and onto transformations
- invertibility

So once one of these properties is known, all the others follow immediately.

### Example

If a $3\times 3$ matrix has a pivot in every column, then:

- the columns are linearly independent
- $A\mathbf{x}=0$ has only the trivial solution
- the transformation $T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}$ is one-to-one
- because the matrix is square, it also has a pivot in every row
- therefore it is onto and invertible

This is exactly the kind of shortcut the theorem gives us.
