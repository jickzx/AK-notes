## 4.3 Linear Transformations

### Definition

A transformation

$$
T:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m
$$

is a [[Linear Transformation|linear transformation]] if, for all vectors $\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\in\mathbb{R}^n$ and all scalars $c$,

$$
T(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})=T(\mathbf{u})+T(\mathbf{v})
$$

and

$$
T(c\mathbf{u})=cT(\mathbf{u}).
$$

These are the two defining properties of linearity.

### Consequences

Every linear transformation satisfies:

$$
T(\mathbf{0})=\mathbf{0}.
$$

So a transformation that does not send the zero vector to the zero vector cannot be linear.

### Matrix Transformations Are Linear

Every matrix transformation

$$
T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}
$$

is linear.

This is because matrix multiplication respects both addition and scalar multiplication.

### Every Linear Transformation Comes from a Matrix

If

$$
T:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m
$$

is linear, then there is a unique matrix $A$ such that

$$
T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}
$$

for all $\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^n$.

The columns of $A$ are

$$
T(\mathbf{e}_1),T(\mathbf{e}_2),\dots,T(\mathbf{e}_n),
$$

where $\mathbf{e}_1,\dots,\mathbf{e}_n$ are the standard basis vectors.

This matrix is called the standard matrix of $T$.

### Kernel and Image

If $T:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$ is linear, then we define

$$
\ker(T)=\{\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^n \mid T(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{0}\}
$$

and

$$
\operatorname{im}(T)=\{T(\mathbf{x})\mid \mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^n\}.
$$

These are called the [[Kernel|kernel]] and image of $T$.

If $T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}$ is a matrix transformation, then

$$
\ker(T)=\operatorname{Nul}(A)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\operatorname{im}(T)=\operatorname{Col}(A).
$$

So the kernel is the linear-map version of the [[Null Space|null space]].

### Recipe: Find the Standard Matrix

To compute the standard matrix of a linear transformation $T$:

1. Apply $T$ to each standard basis vector $\mathbf{e}_1,\dots,\mathbf{e}_n$.
2. Place the resulting vectors as the columns of a matrix.

### Example

Suppose

$$
T:\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}^2,
\qquad
T\left(
\begin{pmatrix}
x\\
y
\end{pmatrix}
\right)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
x+2y\\
3x-y
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
T(\mathbf{e}_1)=
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
3
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
T(\mathbf{e}_2)=
\begin{pmatrix}
2\\
-1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

So the standard matrix is

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2\\
3 & -1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

### Identity Matrix

The standard matrix of the identity transformation on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is the identity matrix $I_n.$
