## 4.2 One-to-one and Onto Transformations

### One-to-one Transformations

#### Definition

A transformation

$$
T:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m
$$

is [[One-to-one Transformation|one-to-one]] if, for every $\mathbf{b}\in\mathbb{R}^m$, the equation

$$
T(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{b}
$$

has at most one solution $\mathbf{x}$.

Equivalent ways to say this:

- different inputs give different outputs
- if $T(\mathbf{u})=T(\mathbf{v})$, then $\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{v}$

For a matrix transformation $T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}$, this is equivalent to:

- $A\mathbf{x}=0$ has only the trivial solution
- the columns of $A$ are [[Linear Independence|linearly independent]]
- $A$ has a [[Pivot|pivot]] in every column

### Onto Transformations

#### Definition

A transformation

$$
T:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m
$$

is [[Onto Transformation|onto]] if every vector in the codomain is actually an output.

So $T$ is onto if, for every $\mathbf{b}\in\mathbb{R}^m$, the equation

$$
T(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{b}
$$

has at least one solution.

For a matrix transformation $T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}$, this is equivalent to:

- $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ is [[Consistent System|consistent]] for every $\mathbf{b}\in\mathbb{R}^m$
- the columns of $A$ span $\mathbb{R}^m$
- $A$ has a pivot in every row

### Example

Let

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0\\
0 & 1 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
T:\mathbb{R}^3\to\mathbb{R}^2,
\qquad
T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}.
$$

The transformation is onto because $A$ has a pivot in every row, but it is not one-to-one because there is a free variable.

### Square Matrices

If $A$ is an $n\times n$ matrix, then for $T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}$:

$$
T \text{ is one-to-one } \Longleftrightarrow T \text{ is onto.}
$$

This is because a square matrix has a pivot in every row if and only if it has a pivot in every column.
