## 4.4 Matrix Multiplication

### Composition of Transformations

If

$$
T:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
S:\mathbb{R}^m\to\mathbb{R}^p,
$$

then their composition is

$$
(S\circ T)(\mathbf{x})=S(T(\mathbf{x})).
$$

If $T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}$ and $S(\mathbf{x})=B\mathbf{x}$, then

$$
(S\circ T)(\mathbf{x})=B(A\mathbf{x})=(BA)\mathbf{x}.
$$

So matrix multiplication corresponds to composition of matrix transformations.

### Definition of Matrix Multiplication

If $A$ is an $m\times n$ matrix and $B$ is an $n\times p$ matrix, then the product $AB$ is defined and is an $m\times p$ matrix.

The $j$th column of $AB$ is

$$
A\mathbf{b}_j,
$$

where $\mathbf{b}_j$ is the $j$th column of $B$.

Equivalently, the $(i,j)$ entry of $AB$ is the dot product of row $i$ of $A$ with column $j$ of $B$.

### Example

Let

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2\\
0 & 1
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B=
\begin{pmatrix}
3 & 1\\
4 & -1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
AB=
\begin{pmatrix}
11 & -1\\
4 & -1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

### Important Properties

Whenever the products are defined:

$$
C(A+B)=CA+CB
$$

$$
(A+B)C=AC+BC
$$

$$
(AB)C=A(BC)
$$

$$
A I_n = A,
\qquad
I_m A = A
$$

In general,

$$
AB \ne BA.
$$

So matrix multiplication is not commutative.

### Why This Matters

Matrix multiplication lets us combine several linear processes into a single matrix.

That is why it becomes the algebraic language for working with [[Linear Transformation|linear transformations]].
