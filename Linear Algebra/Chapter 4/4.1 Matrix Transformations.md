## 4.1 Matrix Transformations

### Matrices as Functions

Let $A$ be an $m\times n$ matrix. Then the rule

$$
T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}
$$

defines a [[Matrix Transformation|matrix transformation]]

$$
T:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m.
$$

So a matrix can be viewed as a function:

- the input is a vector $\mathbf{x}$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$
- the output is the vector $A\mathbf{x}$ in $\mathbb{R}^m$

### Vocabulary

- The [[Domain|domain]] is the set of allowed inputs.
- The [[Codomain|codomain]] is the space where outputs live.
- The [[Range|range]] is the set of actual outputs.

For a matrix transformation $T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}$:

- domain: $\mathbb{R}^n$
- codomain: $\mathbb{R}^m$
- range: $\operatorname{Col}(A)$

### Geometric Meaning

The equation

$$
\mathbf{b}=A\mathbf{x}
$$

can be interpreted as saying that $\mathbf{b}$ is the output produced from the input $\mathbf{x}$.

So instead of thinking only about solving equations, we can think of $A$ as moving vectors from one space to another.

### Common Examples

Typical matrix transformations in $\mathbb{R}^2$ include:

- reflections
- rotations
- dilations
- shears
- projections

Each is represented by multiplying by a particular matrix.

### Example: Reflection Across the $x$-Axis

The matrix

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0\\
0 & -1
\end{pmatrix}
$$

defines the transformation

$$
T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}.
$$

If

$$
\mathbf{x}=
\begin{pmatrix}
x\\
y
\end{pmatrix},
$$

then

$$
T(\mathbf{x})=
\begin{pmatrix}
x\\
-y
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

So this reflects vectors across the $x$-axis.

### Identity Transformation

The identity transformation on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is

$$
\operatorname{Id}_{\mathbb{R}^n}(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{x}.
$$

Its matrix is the identity matrix

$$
I_n.
$$
