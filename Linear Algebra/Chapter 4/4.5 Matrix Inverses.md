## 4.5 Matrix Inverses

### Invertible Matrices

#### Definition

A square matrix $A$ is [[Inverse Matrix|invertible]] if there exists a matrix $A^{-1}$ such that

$$
A^{-1}A=I
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
AA^{-1}=I.
$$

The matrix $A^{-1}$ is called the inverse of $A$.

### Solving Matrix Equations

If $A$ is invertible, then the equation

$$
A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}
$$

has the unique solution

$$
\mathbf{x}=A^{-1}\mathbf{b}.
$$

So an inverse lets us "divide by $A$" when solving a matrix equation.

### Example

Let

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
2 & 1\\
1 & 1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Then

$$
A^{-1}=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & -1\\
-1 & 2
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

So the solution of

$$
A\mathbf{x}=
\begin{pmatrix}
3\\
2
\end{pmatrix}
$$

is

$$
\mathbf{x}=A^{-1}
\begin{pmatrix}
3\\
2
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
1
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

### Invertible Transformations

If $T(\mathbf{x})=A\mathbf{x}$ and $A$ is invertible, then $T$ is an invertible transformation.

Its inverse is

$$
T^{-1}(\mathbf{x})=A^{-1}\mathbf{x}.
$$

So invertible matrices and invertible matrix transformations are two versions of the same idea.

### Computing the Inverse

To compute $A^{-1}$, row reduce the augmented matrix

$$
\left(
\begin{array}{c|c}
A & I
\end{array}
\right)
$$

until the left side becomes $I$.

If this succeeds, the right side becomes $A^{-1}$.

If the left side cannot be reduced to $I$, then $A$ is not invertible.

#### Example

Let

$$
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 2\\
3 & 4
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Start with

$$
\left(
\begin{array}{cc|cc}
1 & 2 & 1 & 0\\
3 & 4 & 0 & 1
\end{array}
\right).
$$

Row reducing gives

$$
\left(
\begin{array}{cc|cc}
1 & 0 & -2 & 1\\
0 & 1 & \tfrac{3}{2} & -\tfrac{1}{2}
\end{array}
\right),
$$

so

$$
A^{-1}=
\begin{pmatrix}
-2 & 1\\
\tfrac{3}{2} & -\tfrac{1}{2}
\end{pmatrix}.
$$
