## 4.7 Invertible Matrices and Coordinate Systems

### Bases and Invertible Matrices

Let

$$
B=
\begin{pmatrix}
| & | & & |\\
\mathbf{v}_1 & \mathbf{v}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{v}_n\\
| & | & & |
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The columns form a basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$ if and only if $B$ is invertible.

So an invertible matrix can be thought of as a basis written in standard coordinates.

### Converting Coordinates

If the columns of $B$ form a basis $B=(\mathbf{v}_1,\dots,\mathbf{v}_n)$, then for any vector $\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^n$:

$$
\mathbf{x}=B[\mathbf{x}]_B.
$$

Therefore,

$$
[\mathbf{x}]_B=B^{-1}\mathbf{x}.
$$

So:

- $B$ converts $B$-coordinates to standard coordinates
- $B^{-1}$ converts standard coordinates to $B$-coordinates

### Linear Transformations Relative to Bases

Suppose

$$
T:V\to W
$$

is a linear transformation, and $B$ is a basis for $V$ while $C$ is a basis for $W$.

The matrix of $T$ relative to these bases is the matrix $[T]_{C\leftarrow B}$ whose columns are the $C$-coordinates of

$$
T(\mathbf{v}_1),\dots,T(\mathbf{v}_n).
$$

So the matrix records how $T$ acts after we express vectors in the chosen coordinate systems.

### Recipe: Compute the $(B,C)$-Matrix

To compute the matrix of $T$ relative to bases $B$ and $C$:

1. Apply $T$ to each basis vector in $B$.
2. Write each output in $C$-coordinates.
3. Use those coordinate vectors as the columns of the matrix.

### Example

Let

$$
B=
\left(
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix},
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
\right)
$$

be a basis of $\mathbb{R}^2$, and let

$$
\mathbf{x}=
\begin{pmatrix}
3\\
5
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Since

$$
\mathbf{x}
=
-2
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
0
\end{pmatrix}
+
5
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
1
\end{pmatrix},
$$

we have

$$
[\mathbf{x}]_B=
\begin{pmatrix}
-2\\
5
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

This illustrates how coordinates depend on the chosen basis.

### Why This Matters

Different bases can simplify the description of a linear transformation.

That is why coordinates relative to a basis are such an important bridge between abstract vector spaces and matrix computations.
