### When to Flip Arguments:

- **Symmetry**: If the predicate P(x,y)P(x, y)P(x,y) is symmetric, flipping is trivial and doesn't affect the proof.
- **Instantiating quantifiers**: You may flip arguments when you instantiate quantifiers (e.g., ∀y∈U,P(x,y)\forall y \in U, P(x, y)∀y∈U,P(x,y)) to match the specific elements you are working with in the proof.
- **Proof by contradiction**: When trying to derive a contradiction, flipping arguments can often reveal logical inconsistencies.
- **Fit a logical structure**: If you're transforming the statement into a more useful form for applying a lemma or another theorem.
- **Reversing direction**: If you're proving an equivalence or using the contrapositive of an implication.

### Conclusion:

You flip the arguments of a predicate when it's necessary for:

- **Handling asymmetry** in relations.
- **Applying quantifiers** to specific instances in the proof.
- **Deriving contradictions** in a proof by contradiction.
- **Manipulating logical structures** to match the form of the statement you're proving.

Flipping is often a tool to control the flow of the proof and is used strategically to reveal contradictions, apply known results, or fit the logical form needed for the argument.
